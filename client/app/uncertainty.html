Uncertainty

Sources of uncertainty in these data

These data represent only “snapshots” in time, and historical trends in Arctic sea ice cover and extent. They are not projections or predictions of future conditions. That said, we acknowledge several sources of uncertainty and offer these sea ice data for analysis and download with these caveats:

Data collection challenges.  Collecting sea ice data is difficult: traveling through or near sea ice can be perilous or impossible for most vessels, and was especially dangerous for historic voyagers. Another hazard is the weather, which can be severe for observers aboard planes or ships. Historical data collection methods and interpretations of ice concentration often differed from modern protocols, and observers over time have used different types of sensors (e.g., radar, satellites) and instrument calibrations. All of these add up to inconsistencies in the data.

Data compilation challenges.  Compiling data always involves a series of choices. Experts do their best to answer questions such as: Which hard copy maps to digitize? How do we interpret this handwriting? How do we fill gaps in the data record? Which time scale is best to use? These choices lead to uncertainty.

It is important to realize that uncertainty is not an enemy of science that holds it back – it is a stimulus that drives it forward.

The role of science is to assess the evidence and show which of several possible answers has the most support. Where uncertainties remain, scientists know what to investigate next. We all make decisions in our daily lives that are based on incomplete information. It is helpful to know about uncertainties up front so that we can make informed decisions.
